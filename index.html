    1. Ownership and Control

      • Web1:

        Also known as the "read-only" web, Web1 was primarily about consuming content rather than creating it. Websites were static, and the interaction was minimal, typically limited to reading information on web pages. Users couldn't interact much with the content, and the control lay largely with website owners and content creators.

      • Web2:

        Often referred to as the "read-write" web, Web2 introduced interactivity, social networking, and user-generated content. Platforms like Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter allowed users to interact and contribute content, making the web much more dynamic. However, the control and ownership of data were (and are) largely in the hands of centralized entities that own these platforms.

      • Web3:

        Sometimes called the "read-write-trust" web, Web3 adds a layer of decentralized protocols, primarily using blockchain technology. This evolution aims to give users ownership and control over their own data, often through the use of cryptocurrencies and non-fungible tokens (NFTs). In Web3, services are decentralized, and interactions can happen on peer-to-peer networks without central authorities.

    2. Technology Infrastructure:

      • Web1:

        Built on static HTML pages that were served from servers over the Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP). This era was defined by hyperlinks and web directories that helped users navigate the web.

      • Web2:

        Marked by the rise of the mobile internet, broadband, and cloud computing. Technologies like AJAX and HTML5 allowed for the creation of interactive web applications and improved multimedia experiences.

      • Web3:

        Relies on blockchain technology, smart contracts, and other cryptographic technologies. This setup not only facilitates transactions but also supports more complex interactions like those required by decentralized applications (dApps).

    3. Economic Models

      • Web1:

        The economic model was primarily based on advertising and straightforward e-commerce (selling goods and services directly through websites).

      • Web2:

        Dominated by data monetization, where user data is often collected, analyzed, and used for targeted advertising, creating significant revenue for platform providers.

      • Web3:

        Introduces new economic models based on tokenomics, where value is generated and distributed among users through tokens. These models encourage user participation and contribution, as stakeholders can earn tokens through various activities like governance, content creation, and platform support.
